Hale Ulu a Ho'ola

(House of Growth and Healing)

A clean and sober transitional living program for Hawai'i youth

Our Kuleana 

(Our Responsibility/Privilege)

Guiding Hawaii's young adults aged 18-25 toward sobriety and holistic wellness, our clean and sober house is a haven of support and growth. Embracing the healing vibes of Hawaii, we offer comprehensive services including housing assistance, education, employment support, legal advocacy, and native cultural practices. Grounded in the spirit of aloha, we nurture personal empowerment and resilience, fostering a community where each individual can thrive, embodying the true essence of living pono.

We welcome you with "Oli Mahalo - Uhola 'ia" is a Hawaiian phrase. "Oli" means chant or prayer of thanksgiving, "Mahalo" means gratitude or thanks, and "Uhola 'ia" translates to being released or set free. So, altogether, "Oli Mahalo - Uhola 'ia" could be interpreted as a chant or prayer of gratitude for being released or set free. It might be used in various contexts, such as expressing thankfulness for overcoming obstacles, achieving freedom, or experiencing liberation from something.

Hawaiian Values

  • Aloha: caring, compassion for others, love, affection.

  • Ha`aha`a: humility, humbleness, modesty.

  • Ho`omau: preserve, perpetuate, continue.

  • `Ike Pono: to know, to feel, to understand.

  • Kokua: help, assist, comfort, support.

  • Kuleana: privilege, responsibility, title, job.

  • Kupa`a: stand firm, steadfast.

Mālama ʻĀina

(to care for the land)

We go into a Lo'i (Taro/Kalo farm) and help cultivate the land. In Hawaiian culture, "aina" refers not only to the physical land but also to the entire environment, encompassing the land, sea, and sky. "Malama" means to nurture, preserve, protect, and sustain. So, "Malama Aina" encapsulates the concept of environmental stewardship and emphasizes the importance of respecting and caring for the natural world. It involves practices that promote sustainability, conservation, and harmony with nature, reflecting a deep cultural and spiritual connection to the land; fostering unity and growth in their healing journey.

Building an Imu

(traditional underground oven)

Building an imu, a traditional Hawaiian underground oven, offers youth in the program a meaningful connection to their culture and heritage, fostering pride and belonging. Engaging in the process teaches valuable life skills, teamwork, and sustainability practices, promoting personal growth. Sharing a meal cooked in the imu creates a communal experience, strengthening bonds among participants and facilitating open dialogue, support, and healing within the group.

We believe that by merging the path to recovery with the rich cultural heritage of the islands this offers young adults a supportive environment to embrace sobriety, while also engaging them in a variety of cultural activities that deepen their connection to the community and the vibrant Hawaiian culture. By participating in Native Hawaiian practices, such as building and cooking in an imu, lauhala weaving, and working the lo'i; residents not only learn about the island's history and traditions but also find a sense of belonging and identity within the community. This innovative approach fosters a holistic recovery journey, where healing is not just about abstaining from substances but also about building meaningful relationships, healing past trauma, understanding oneself through the lens of culture, and contributing positively to the wider community. Such a house stands as a beacon of hope, offering a pathway to not just sobriety, but to a fulfilled and engaged life amidst the beauty and spirit of Hawaii.
